This is a training terminal. Every attack below is explained so you can defend against it — on your computer, your phone, your network, and the websites you build. For each threat you get three things: what it is, why it works (the actual mechanism the attacker abuses), and how to shut it down. Later sections add the real history — who wrote the world's most infamous malware and why — and how anonymity tools and the underground hacking scene actually began.

One idea ties the whole terminal together: computers do exactly what they're told, at the privilege they're told to use, and they trust whatever speaks the right protocol. Almost every attack is just an abuse of misplaced trust — in a file, a message, a network, a login, or a line of input. Defense is the discipline of verifying before you trust.

⚠ ETHICS PROTOCOL: This material is for learning and defense only. Test only on systems you own or have written permission to test. Attacking systems you don't own is a crime in most of the world. Understand attacks to protect people — not to harm them.
01COMPUTER · MALWARE

Malware ("malicious software") is any code written to run against your interests. The categories below describe behavior — a single real-world sample often combines several (a trojan that drops a rootkit that installs a keylogger). Learn the behaviors and you can reason about anything new.

VIRUS

self-replicating

Attaches to a real file or program. When you open the host, the virus code runs too and copies itself into other files. It cannot spread on its own — it needs you to run the infected host.

WHY IT WORKS: It rides a file you trust. Once you double-click, the OS can't tell "your document" from "your document + hidden code."
DEFENSE: Don't run untrusted files; keep antivirus + OS updated; enable "show file extensions" so photo.jpg.exe can't hide.

WORM

self-spreading, no host

Standalone code that copies itself across a network on its own — no click required — by exploiting an unpatched service. One vulnerable machine infects the next automatically.

WHY IT WORKS: It abuses a known software bug on machines that were never patched. Spread is exponential once it starts.
DEFENSE: Patch fast, segment networks, close unused ports, run a firewall. Worms starve on a patched network.

TROJAN

disguise

Malware wearing a useful costume — a "free" game, a cracked app, a fake installer or update. Does its advertised job while quietly doing the attacker's.

WHY IT WORKS: Pure social engineering — you chose to install it, so every OS warning was already dismissed by you.
DEFENSE: Install only from official sources; verify signatures/hashes; treat "free cracked software" as bait.

RANSOMWARE

extortion

Encrypts your files with a key only the attacker holds, then demands payment (usually crypto) to decrypt. Modern strains also steal a copy first and threaten to leak it ("double extortion").

WHY IT WORKS: Strong encryption is irreversible without the key. It weaponizes real cryptography against you, and panic pushes victims to pay.
DEFENSE: Offline + versioned backups (the one real cure), least-privilege accounts, and fast patching. Backups make the ransom pointless.

SPYWARE / KEYLOGGER

surveillance

Silently records keystrokes, screenshots, passwords, camera/mic and browsing — then exfiltrates them to the attacker.

WHY IT WORKS: It runs at your privilege level, so it can legitimately read what you type. Nothing looks "broken," so you never notice.
DEFENSE: 2FA (a stolen password alone won't log in), a password manager (autofill defeats keyloggers), EDR/antimalware, camera covers.

ROOTKIT / BOOTKIT

stealth + persistence

Buries itself deep — kernel, bootloader, or firmware — to hide other malware and survive reboots and even OS reinstalls.

WHY IT WORKS: It runs below the tools you'd use to detect it. The OS itself is compromised and lies to you about what's running.
DEFENSE: Secure Boot, signed drivers, firmware updates. If confirmed, wipe and reimage — you can't trust a rooted system.

BOTNET AGENT

remote control

Turns your machine into a soldier in an army of infected devices, remotely controlled for DDoS, spam, credential stuffing, or crypto-mining.

WHY IT WORKS: Owners rarely notice a little extra CPU or traffic. Power comes from scale — millions of quiet, unpatched devices.
DEFENSE: Patch, change default passwords (especially IoT/routers), monitor outbound traffic for odd connections.

FILELESS / LIVING-OFF-THE-LAND

no file to scan

Runs entirely in memory using trusted built-in tools (PowerShell, scripting engines, macros) so there's no malicious file on disk to detect.

WHY IT WORKS: Classic antivirus scans files — this leaves none — and it abuses legitimate admin tools, so activity looks "normal."
DEFENSE: Behavior-based EDR, script-block logging, restrict/monitor PowerShell, disable Office macros by default, least privilege.

ADWARE / PUP

nuisance + tracking

Floods you with ads, hijacks your browser/search, and quietly profiles you. "Potentially Unwanted Programs" ride along with free installers.

WHY IT WORKS: Bundled installers pre-check "extras," and users click Next-Next-Finish without reading.
DEFENSE: Choose "custom install" and untick extras; use reputable ad/tracker blockers; remove unknown browser extensions.

WIPER

pure destruction

Looks like ransomware but there is no key and no recovery — its only goal is to destroy data and systems. Often used in sabotage and cyber-warfare.

WHY IT WORKS: Victims waste time trying to "pay" or "decrypt" while the damage is already permanent.
DEFENSE: Immutable offline backups, network segmentation, and treating any "ransom" as unrecoverable — restore, don't negotiate.

02MALWARE · ORIGINS & HISTORY

Malware didn't start as crime — it started as curiosity. The story runs from harmless lab experiments, to teenage pranks, to organized crime, to nation-state cyber-weapons. Knowing who built the famous ones and why reveals how motives escalated from "can this be done?" to "this can wreck a country."

1971
CREEPER — the first self-replicating program

Written by Bob Thomas at BBN as an experiment on ARPANET (the early internet). It hopped between machines printing "I'm the creeper, catch me if you can." It was harmless. A follow-up program, Reaper, was written to chase and delete it — arguably the first "antivirus."

WHO: Bob Thomas (BBN)WHY: research / proof of concept
1982
ELK CLONER — first virus "in the wild"

Coded by Rich Skrenta, a 15-year-old, as a prank for the Apple II. It spread via floppy disks and displayed a little poem every 50th boot. Annoying, not destructive — but the first virus to escape a lab and spread among real users.

WHO: Rich Skrenta, age 15WHY: prank
1986
BRAIN — first PC virus

Written by brothers Basit and Amjad Farooq Alvi in Lahore, Pakistan, who ran a computer shop. They say they built it to track illegal copying of medical software they'd written. It even contained their real names, address, and phone number. It became the first virus for the IBM PC.

WHO: the Alvi brothersWHY: anti-piracy tracking
1988
THE MORRIS WORM — the internet's first big outage

Robert Tappan Morris, a Cornell grad student, released a worm meant (he said) to measure the size of the internet. A bug made it re-infect machines over and over, crashing thousands of computers — roughly 10% of the internet at the time. He became the first person convicted under the U.S. Computer Fraud and Abuse Act, and later became an MIT professor.

WHO: Robert T. MorrisWHY: curiosity → accidental damage
2000
ILOVEYOU — the love-letter worm

Onel de Guzman, a student in the Philippines, unleashed a worm that arrived as an email with the subject "ILOVEYOU" and a fake love-letter attachment. Opening it overwrote files and mailed itself to everyone in your address book. He later said he wanted to steal passwords for free internet access. It hit tens of millions of machines; the Philippines had no cybercrime law yet, so charges were dropped.

WHO: Onel de GuzmanWHY: steal internet passwords
2004
MYDOOM & SASSER — the record-breakers

Mydoom became the fastest-spreading email worm ever recorded, still often cited as the costliest in history (rough estimates near $38B). Its author was never confirmed. The same year, German teenager Sven Jaschan (17) wrote Sasser and Netsky, worms that exploited a Windows flaw to spread with no user action; a Microsoft bounty led to his arrest.

WHO: Mydoom — unknown · Sasser — Sven Jaschan (17)WHY: disruption / notoriety
2007
ZEUS — malware becomes a business

A banking trojan built to steal online-banking logins at massive scale. Zeus was sold and rented in criminal markets, spawning countless variants and a whole underground economy. This marks the shift from fame-seeking to professional, for-profit cybercrime.

WHO: organized cybercrimeWHY: money — bank fraud
2010
STUXNET — the first cyber-weapon

A staggeringly complex worm widely attributed to a joint U.S.–Israel operation. It targeted the specific Siemens industrial controllers running Iran's Natanz uranium centrifuges and made them tear themselves apart while reporting "all normal" to operators. Stuxnet is the first known malware to cause real-world physical destruction, using several previously-unknown "zero-day" exploits.

WHO: attributed to nation-statesWHY: sabotage a nuclear program
2013
CRYPTOLOCKER — the ransomware boom

Popularized the modern crypto-ransomware model: strong encryption plus anonymous cryptocurrency payment. Its "success" spawned an entire ransomware industry, later evolving into "ransomware-as-a-service" rented to affiliates.

WHO: organized cybercrimeWHY: extortion for profit
2016
MIRAI — the internet-of-things army

Written by three college-age friends (Paras Jha, Josiah White, Dalton Norman), Mirai hijacked cheap IoT devices — cameras, routers, DVRs — that still used factory-default passwords. It started as a scheme to attack Minecraft servers, then took down the DNS provider Dyn in October 2016, knocking Twitter, Netflix, Reddit and others offline. They published the source code, so copycats spread everywhere. All three pleaded guilty.

WHO: Jha, White, NormanWHY: DDoS-for-hire / gaming rivalry
2017
WANNACRY & NOTPETYA — weapons go global

WannaCry was a ransomware worm (attributed to North Korea) that spread using EternalBlue, a Windows exploit leaked from a stolen NSA toolkit. It crippled hospitals, telecoms and factories across 150+ countries until a researcher found an accidental "kill switch."

NotPetya (attributed to Russian military hackers) looked like ransomware but was really a wiper, spread by hijacking a Ukrainian tax-software update. It caused an estimated $10B in damage worldwide — often called the most destructive cyberattack in history.

WHO: attributed to nation-statesWHY: disruption / geopolitics

CHART: estimated global damage of infamous malware

Dollar figures for malware are notoriously rough — they blend cleanup, downtime and lost productivity, and estimates vary widely between sources. Treat the bars below as orders of magnitude, not precise accounting.

THE PATTERN: notice the motive drift — curiosity (1971) → showing off (1982-2004) → money (2005+) → statecraft (2010+). Defenders win by making each stage harder: patch to kill worms, back up to kill ransomware, verify updates to kill supply-chain attacks.
03PROTECT · ANTI-MALWARE PLAYBOOK

No single tool stops malware. Real protection is defense in depth — layers, so that when one fails the next still catches the attack. Below: the universal layers, then a targeted counter for each malware type from section 01.

THE 10 UNIVERSAL LAYERS

  • Update everything, automatically. OS, browser, apps, routers, IoT. Most malware exploits bugs that already have a fix — patching is the single highest-value habit.
  • Run as a standard user, not admin/root. Least privilege limits what any malware can do the moment it runs.
  • Keep offline, versioned backups (3-2-1 rule). 3 copies, 2 media, 1 off-site/offline. This is the only guaranteed cure for ransomware and wipers.
  • Use reputable anti-malware / EDR that watches behavior, not just known file signatures — that's what catches fileless and new threats.
  • Enable a firewall and close ports/services you don't use. Fewer open doors = fewer worm targets.
  • Turn on MFA everywhere and use a password manager with unique passwords. Even if malware steals a password, MFA blocks the login.
  • Only install from trusted sources; verify signatures/hashes. No cracked software, no random "codec" downloads.
  • Disable Office macros by default; don't enable "content" in documents from email.
  • Block ads/trackers & suspicious sites; a lot of malware arrives through malicious ads ("malvertising").
  • Slow down on urgency. "Act now or lose access" is the fingerprint of social engineering. Verify before you click.

TARGETED COUNTERS — ONE PER MALWARE TYPE

vs VIRUS

  • Never run untrusted executables or macros.
  • Show file extensions; beware .exe/.scr/.js in disguise.
  • On-access antivirus scanning enabled.

vs WORM

  • Patch network-facing services fast.
  • Segment networks; isolate IoT and guests.
  • Firewall off unused ports/SMB.

vs TROJAN

  • Official app stores / vendor sites only.
  • Verify code signatures and hashes.
  • Never "crack" paid software.

vs RANSOMWARE

  • Offline, versioned, tested backups.
  • Least privilege + rapid patching.
  • Block macros & malicious email attachments.

vs SPYWARE / KEYLOGGER

  • MFA so stolen creds are useless.
  • Password manager autofill beats key-logging.
  • Review app permissions; cover camera/mic.

vs ROOTKIT / BOOTKIT

  • Secure Boot + signed drivers only.
  • Keep firmware/UEFI updated.
  • If confirmed: wipe and reimage from clean media.

vs BOTNET AGENT

  • Change ALL default passwords (routers/IoT).
  • Monitor outbound traffic for odd calls home.
  • Keep firmware current; disable UPnP if unused.

vs FILELESS

  • Behavior-based EDR, not signatures alone.
  • Restrict & log PowerShell/scripting.
  • Least privilege blocks escalation.

vs ADWARE / PUP

  • Custom install; untick bundled "extras."
  • Audit and remove browser extensions.
  • Reputable ad/tracker blocker.

vs WIPER

  • Immutable, offline backups (assume no recovery).
  • Segment so one host can't reach everything.
  • Rehearsed incident-response & restore plan.
IF YOU'RE ALREADY INFECTED: disconnect from the network, don't pay ransoms blindly, preserve evidence, wipe and reimage from known-clean media, rotate every password from a clean device, and re-enable MFA. For anything serious, involve professionals / law enforcement rather than "cleaning" a compromised machine.
04PHONE · MOBILE THREATS

Your phone is a wallet, a key, a camera, and a tracking beacon in one. Mobile attacks mostly abuse two things: permissions you grant too fast, and trust in messages that look official. Phones are also harder to inspect than PCs, so bad apps hide longer.

MALICIOUS / OVER-PERMISSIONED APPS

sideloading & permissions

A flashlight app that wants your contacts, mic, and location. Sideloaded APKs and fake "mod" apps can carry anything.

WHY IT WORKS: People tap "Allow" on autopilot. Once granted, the permission is legitimate — the OS now lets the app do exactly what you approved.
DEFENSE: Install from official stores; read permissions ("does a flashlight need my contacts?"); revoke unused permissions; prefer apps with clear privacy info.

SMISHING & VISHING

social engineering

Phishing by SMS ("parcel held — pay here") or voice call ("this is your bank's fraud team"). A fake link or a panicked call harvests logins, codes, or card details.

WHY IT WORKS: Sender IDs and caller IDs are easy to spoof, screens show short links, and urgency switches off skepticism.
DEFENSE: Never tap links or read codes to callers. Hang up and dial the number on the back of your card. Open the real app yourself.

ROGUE / PUBLIC WI-FI

network trust

"Free_Airport_WiFi" run by an attacker can watch or redirect your traffic.

WHY IT WORKS: Devices auto-join familiar names, and traffic that isn't encrypted is readable by whoever runs the access point.
DEFENSE: Rely on HTTPS (default now), use a trusted VPN on public Wi-Fi, and turn off auto-join for open networks.

SIM SWAPPING

account takeover

An attacker convinces your carrier to move your number to their SIM, then intercepts your SMS 2FA codes and password resets.

WHY IT WORKS: SMS 2FA assumes "your number = you," and carrier support staff can be socially engineered into porting it.
DEFENSE: Use app-based or hardware 2FA instead of SMS; set a carrier port-out PIN; don't post your number publicly.

OUTDATED OS & ZERO-CLICK

unpatched exploits

Old phone software has publicly-known holes. The worst — "zero-click" spyware like commercial surveillance kits — can infect a phone with no tap at all via a crafted message.

WHY IT WORKS: Once a patch ships, the bug it fixed is public. Unpatched phones are documented open doors; high-end spyware buys unknown ones.
DEFENSE: Auto-update; buy phones with long support windows; enable "Lockdown"-style hardened modes if you're high-risk; retire unsupported devices.

MALICIOUS QR / BLUETOOTH / NFC

proximity vectors

A QR code sends you to a phishing page ("quishing"); open Bluetooth/NFC can be probed by nearby attackers.

WHY IT WORKS: You can't read a QR with your eyes, so you trust wherever it points; radios left discoverable advertise themselves.
DEFENSE: Preview QR URLs before opening; don't scan random codes; keep Bluetooth/NFC off or non-discoverable when unused.
05NETWORK ATTACKS

Networks were built for connectivity first and security second. Many core protocols simply trust whoever speaks the right message — with no proof of identity. Attackers slip into that gap to listen, redirect, or impersonate.

PACKET SNIFFING

eavesdropping

Capturing raw traffic on a network to read anything sent in the clear.

WHY IT WORKS: On shared/open networks data is broadcast, and unencrypted protocols (HTTP, plain DNS) expose credentials in plaintext.
DEFENSE: Encrypt everything — HTTPS/TLS, VPN, SSH. Sniffed ciphertext is useless without the key.

MITM (MAN-IN-THE-MIDDLE)

interception

Attacker sits between you and the destination, relaying and reading (or altering) traffic.

WHY IT WORKS: If nothing verifies who you're really talking to, an interceptor can impersonate both sides at once.
DEFENSE: Valid TLS certificates + heed browser warnings; certificate pinning; never click through "connection not private."

ARP SPOOFING

LAN poisoning

Attacker lies about which hardware address owns an IP, so local traffic flows through them.

WHY IT WORKS: ARP has no authentication — devices simply believe the last "I'm the router" reply they hear.
DEFENSE: Dynamic ARP Inspection on switches, static ARP for critical hosts, and encryption so intercepted traffic is unreadable.

DNS SPOOFING

redirection

Feeds you a fake answer for a domain, sending "yourbank.com" to the attacker's server.

WHY IT WORKS: Classic DNS answers aren't signed, so a forged reply that arrives first is accepted as truth.
DEFENSE: DNSSEC, DNS-over-HTTPS/TLS, and HTTPS (the cert won't match the fake server, so you get warned).

EVIL TWIN / ROGUE AP

fake access point

A clone of a real Wi-Fi network with the same name and a stronger signal, luring devices to connect.

WHY IT WORKS: Wi-Fi picks networks by name (SSID), not identity; two networks can share a name and devices prefer the strongest.
DEFENSE: Verify networks, disable auto-join to open SSIDs, use enterprise Wi-Fi auth, VPN on anything untrusted.

DEAUTH ATTACK

denial of service

Forged "disconnect" frames kick devices off Wi-Fi — often to push them onto an evil twin.

WHY IT WORKS: Older Wi-Fi management frames were unauthenticated, so a spoofed deauth is obeyed instantly.
DEFENSE: WPA3 and 802.11w (Protected Management Frames) authenticate these frames and stop the attack.

DDoS (BOTNET FLOOD)

availability attack

Thousands of hijacked devices flood a target with traffic until it collapses — how Mirai took down major sites in 2016.

WHY IT WORKS: Any server has finite capacity; overwhelming scale from a botnet exhausts it faster than it can respond.
DEFENSE: DDoS-mitigation/CDN services, rate limiting, over-provisioning, and (as a device owner) not letting your gear join a botnet.

PORT SCANNING / RECON

pre-attack mapping

Probing which services a host exposes — the reconnaissance step before an exploit.

WHY IT WORKS: Every open, unneeded service is a possible way in, and many are left exposed by default.
DEFENSE: Close/firewall unused ports, expose only what's necessary, and monitor/alert on scanning activity.
06OWASP TOP 10 · WEB

The OWASP Top 10 is the security industry's standard list of the most critical web-application risks, maintained by the non-profit Open Worldwide Application Security Project. Nearly every entry reduces to one sin: trusting input, identity, or configuration that was never verified.

A01

BROKEN ACCESS CONTROL

Users reach data or actions they shouldn't — e.g. changing ?id=123 to ?id=124 and seeing someone else's account. The #1 risk on the current list.

WHY IT WORKS: The server renders a page but never re-checks "is THIS user allowed to touch THIS record?" It trusts the request.
DEFENSE: Enforce authorization server-side on every request; deny by default; never rely on hidden URLs or client-side checks.
A02

CRYPTOGRAPHIC FAILURES

Sensitive data exposed through weak, missing, or misused encryption — plaintext passwords, outdated algorithms, no HTTPS.

WHY IT WORKS: If data isn't properly protected in transit and at rest, one leak or one sniffer exposes everything readable.
DEFENSE: TLS everywhere, modern algorithms, hash passwords with bcrypt/argon2, and don't store data you don't need.
A03

INJECTION (SQL / COMMAND / XSS)

Attacker input is interpreted as code — database queries, OS commands, or scripts running in other users' browsers (cross-site scripting).

WHY IT WORKS: The app builds commands by gluing untrusted text into code, so the interpreter can't tell data from instructions.
DEFENSE: Parameterized queries/prepared statements, output encoding, strict input validation, and a Content-Security-Policy for XSS.
A04

INSECURE DESIGN

The flaw is in the blueprint, not the code — e.g. a password reset with no rate limit, or checkout with no fraud checks.

WHY IT WORKS: Security was never designed in. No amount of clean code fixes a workflow that's insecure by concept.
DEFENSE: Threat-model early, define abuse cases, and build in rate limits and business-logic checks from the design stage.
A05

SECURITY MISCONFIGURATION

Default passwords, verbose error pages, open cloud storage buckets, unnecessary features left switched on.

WHY IT WORKS: Defaults favor convenience over safety, and every extra enabled feature is another door nobody's watching.
DEFENSE: Harden by default, remove unused features, change all defaults, hide detailed errors, automate config review.
A06

VULNERABLE & OUTDATED COMPONENTS

Your app inherits the bugs of every library, framework, and plugin it uses — especially the un-updated ones.

WHY IT WORKS: Known vulnerabilities in popular components are public and mass-scanned for. Old dependency = published exploit.
DEFENSE: Inventory dependencies, patch regularly, use software-composition-analysis scanners, remove unused libraries.
A07

IDENTIFICATION & AUTH FAILURES

Weak logins: no brute-force limits, credential stuffing, guessable sessions, weak password rules.

WHY IT WORKS: If login accepts unlimited guesses and reused passwords, attackers automate their way in with leaked credential lists.
DEFENSE: Multi-factor auth, rate limiting + lockouts, solid session management, check passwords against breach lists.
A08

SOFTWARE & DATA INTEGRITY FAILURES

Trusting code or updates whose integrity isn't verified — poisoned dependencies, unsigned updates, tampered build pipelines (how NotPetya spread).

WHY IT WORKS: If you auto-pull code/updates without verifying the source, a compromised supplier ships malware straight into you.
DEFENSE: Verify digital signatures, pin & check dependency hashes, secure the CI/CD pipeline, review third-party code.
A09

LOGGING & MONITORING FAILURES

Attacks that aren't logged or alerted on go unnoticed for months, giving intruders time to dig in.

WHY IT WORKS: You can't respond to what you can't see. Silence looks identical to safety until it's far too late.
DEFENSE: Log security events, alert on anomalies, centralize logs, and rehearse an incident-response plan.
A10

SSRF (SERVER-SIDE REQUEST FORGERY)

Tricking the server into fetching a URL the attacker chooses — often internal systems the attacker can't reach directly.

WHY IT WORKS: The server fetches user-supplied URLs and is trusted on the internal network, so it becomes the attacker's proxy inside.
DEFENSE: Allow-list permitted destinations, block internal IP ranges, and validate/normalize every URL before fetching.
07CYBERSECURITY · SECURE YOUR COMPUTER

Hardening a machine isn't one setting — it's a stack of small, boring habits that together make you a hard target. Work through these categories; each closes off a class of attack from the sections above.

ACCOUNTS & ACCESS

  • Use a standard user account for daily work, not admin/root.
  • Strong, unique login password or passphrase.
  • Turn on MFA for the OS account and cloud logins.
  • Auto-lock the screen after a short idle; lock it when you walk away.

UPDATES & PATCHING

  • Enable automatic updates for the OS and every app.
  • Update firmware/BIOS and drivers periodically.
  • Uninstall software you don't use — less to patch, less to exploit.

ENCRYPTION & BACKUP

  • Turn on full-disk encryption: BitLocker (Windows), FileVault (macOS), LUKS (Linux).
  • Keep offline, versioned backups (3-2-1) and encrypt them too.
  • Test that you can actually restore a backup.

NETWORK & FIREWALL

  • Keep the OS firewall on; block inbound you don't need.
  • Change your router's default password; use WPA3/WPA2 Wi-Fi.
  • Update router firmware; disable WPS and remote admin.
  • Use a trusted VPN on untrusted networks.

MALWARE DEFENSE

  • Run reputable anti-malware / EDR (behavioral, not just signatures).
  • Disable Office macros by default; don't "enable content" from email.
  • No cracked/pirated software; verify downloads' signatures.
  • Show file extensions so x.jpg.exe can't hide.

PHYSICAL & MISC

  • Enable Secure Boot; set a BIOS/UEFI password.
  • Disable autorun for USB media; be wary of unknown drives.
  • Cover the webcam; be mindful of shoulder-surfing.
  • Wipe drives securely before selling or recycling.
08BROWSER SECURITY

Your browser is the single most-attacked program you run — it executes untrusted code from every site you visit. Lock it down and you shut off phishing, malvertising, and a huge share of everyday attacks.

KEEP IT CURRENT

  • Let the browser auto-update — it patches actively-exploited bugs.
  • Restart when it asks; that's when the fix applies.

ENFORCE HTTPS

  • Turn on HTTPS-Only mode.
  • Never click past "your connection is not private" warnings.
  • Check the domain spelling — lookalikes (paypa1.com) are the whole trick.

EXTENSIONS = RISK

  • Install few, only from official stores, with good reputations.
  • Read the permissions ("read all data on all sites" is a lot).
  • Audit and remove extensions you no longer use.

BLOCK & SANDBOX

  • Use a reputable ad/tracker blocker — stops malvertising.
  • Keep site isolation / sandbox enabled (default in modern browsers).
  • Block third-party cookies.

PASSWORDS & MFA

  • Prefer a dedicated password manager over the browser's saved passwords.
  • Turn on MFA everywhere it's offered.
  • Autofill only on the exact right domain — a mismatch is a phishing tell.

PRIVACY & DNS

  • Enable DNS-over-HTTPS to stop DNS snooping/spoofing.
  • Use a hardened/private profile for sensitive tasks.
  • Turn on Safe Browsing / download protection.
QUICK CHECK: right now, is your browser set to HTTPS-only, do you have an ad-blocker, and are you using a password manager with MFA? Those three cover most day-to-day web risk.
09CODE · HOW IT STARTED & WORKS

Every program — and every piece of malware — is just instructions a machine follows. Understanding how code becomes action demystifies both building software and defending against hostile software.

A SHORT HISTORY OF CODE

1843
The first algorithm

Ada Lovelace wrote what's considered the first computer program — an algorithm for Charles Babbage's (never-built) Analytical Engine — and foresaw that machines could manipulate more than just numbers.

1936
The theory of computation

Alan Turing described the "Turing machine," a mathematical model proving what computers can and can't compute — the foundation of computer science.

1940s-50s
Machine & assembly language

Early computers were programmed in raw binary, then assembly — human-readable shorthand mapped one-to-one to CPU instructions. Grace Hopper's work led to the first compilers.

1957-72
High-level languages

Fortran (1957) let scientists write formulas instead of machine code; C (1972, Dennis Ritchie) became the language that operating systems are built in. Abstraction exploded from here into today's thousands of languages.

HOW CODE ACTUALLY RUNS

The building blocks are the same in every language: variables (store data), conditionals (if/else — make decisions), loops (repeat work), functions (reusable named steps), and data structures (organize information). Master those five ideas and you can read almost any code.

HOW MALWARE IS BUILT — AND WHY WE STUDY DEFENSE

Here's the demystifying truth: malware is ordinary software. It's written in the same languages (C, C++, Python, assembly, and others) using the same variables, loops, and functions as any app. There is no secret "hacker language" — the only difference is malicious intent and the fact it runs without consent.

⚠ This site will not provide working malicious code or a build guide. Writing malware to harm or access systems you don't own is illegal and causes real damage — and you don't need to write it to defend against it. The valuable, legal skill is the defender's side below.

The professional discipline is malware analysis / reverse engineering — the "blue team" craft of understanding hostile code so you can stop it. Analysts safely detonate samples in isolated sandboxes, watch their behavior (files touched, network calls, persistence attempts), and use disassemblers/decompilers to read what the program does. That's how detection signatures, patches, and takedowns get built. Learning to code makes you better at reading these attacks and writing software that resists them (input validation, memory safety, least privilege) — the same secure-coding habits from the OWASP section.

11METHODOLOGY · HOW PROS WORK

Two disciplines power modern tech security: ethical hacking (finding holes before criminals do) and software engineering (building systems that hold up). Both follow repeatable, phased processes.

ETHICAL HACKING / PENETRATION-TEST METHODOLOGY

An ethical hacker (pentester) does everything with written authorization and a defined scope, then reports findings so they can be fixed. That authorization is the entire line between security work and a crime. The widely-taught phases:

1
RECONNAISSANCE

Gather public info about the target's systems and people (footprinting).

2
SCANNING

Map live hosts, open ports, and services; enumerate what's exposed.

3
GAINING ACCESS

Attempt to exploit a weakness — only within the agreed scope.

4
MAINTAINING ACCESS

Assess how deep an attacker could persist and pivot.

5
ANALYSIS & REPORTING

Document findings, risk, and clear fixes; clean up any changes.

SOFTWARE ENGINEERING METHODOLOGY (SDLC)

Software is built through the Software Development Life Cycle — a repeatable path from idea to running system. Security "shifts left," meaning it's built into every phase, not bolted on at the end (that's DevSecOps).

1
PLAN / REQUIREMENTS

Define what to build and the constraints — including threat modeling.

2
DESIGN

Architect the system; choose secure, appropriate patterns.

3
IMPLEMENT

Write the code, following secure-coding standards.

4
TEST

Verify it works and probe for bugs and vulnerabilities.

5
DEPLOY

Release to users, ideally automated and repeatable (CI/CD).

6
MAINTAIN

Monitor, patch, and improve — then loop back to plan.

07ANONYMOUS · TOR · THE UNDERGROUND

Hacker culture is older than the web. It began with curiosity about how systems really work, grew a code of ethics and its own zines, and eventually produced both anonymity tools used by journalists and dissidents — and the masked activist collective the world now pictures when it hears the word "hacker." Here's the factual lineage.

ROOTS — PHREAKING & THE HACKER ETHIC (1960s–1980s)

The word "hack" grew out of MIT's Tech Model Railroad Club in the late 1950s–60s, meaning a clever, playful technical feat. In the 1970s, phone phreaks explored the telephone network: John Draper ("Captain Crunch") found that a toy whistle from a cereal box produced the exact 2600 Hz tone AT&T used to control long-distance lines, enabling free calls. Young Steve Wozniak and Steve Jobs built and sold "blue boxes" that generated those tones — years before founding Apple. The 2600 hacker magazine (1984) took its name from that tone. Journalist Steven Levy codified the community's values as the "Hacker Ethic": information should be free, mistrust authority, judge people by skill, and computers can change life for the better.

GROUPS & ZINES (1980s–1990s)

Organized scenes formed. Germany's Chaos Computer Club (founded 1981) became one of the world's oldest and most influential hacker associations, known for exposing security flaws responsibly. In the U.S., the Cult of the Dead Cow (1984) later coined the term "hacktivism." The e-zine Phrack (1985) published, in 1986, "The Conscience of a Hacker" — better known as The Hacker Manifesto — written by The Mentor (Loyd Blankenship) after his arrest; its line "my crime is that of curiosity" became a cultural touchstone. This era also produced famous figures like Kevin Mitnick, whose 1995 arrest made social engineering household knowledge.

TOR — HOW ANONYMITY ACTUALLY WORKS

Ironically, the most famous anonymity tool was born inside the U.S. government. Onion routing was invented in the mid-1990s at the U.S. Naval Research Laboratory (by Paul Syverson, Michael Reed and David Goldschlag) to protect intelligence communications. In 2002, Roger Dingledine and Nick Mathewson built and released Tor ("The Onion Router"); the non-profit Tor Project was founded in 2006. Tor bounces your traffic through several volunteer relays, wrapping it in layers of encryption like an onion — each relay only knows the previous and next hop, so no single point sees both who you are and what you're doing.

LEGAL vs ILLEGAL: Tor itself is a lawful privacy tool. Like any strong technology it can be misused — the "dark web" also hosted criminal markets (below) — but anonymity is also what protects reporters, whistleblowers, and people under censorship. The tool is neutral; the use isn't.

ANONYMOUS — THE MASKED COLLECTIVE (2003–2010s)

Anonymous grew out of the imageboard 4chan in the mid-2000s, where posts default to the name "Anonymous" — so a leaderless crowd began acting under one identity. Its symbol, the Guy Fawkes mask (from the graphic novel and 2006 film V for Vendetta), became shorthand for faceless collective protest. The turning point was Project Chanology (2008), a campaign against the Church of Scientology after it tried to censor a leaked video — mixing online action with real-world street protests. In 2010, Operation Payback and "Avenge Assange" launched denial-of-service actions against companies that cut off WikiLeaks. A splinter group, LulzSec (2011), ran a high-profile spree before arrests followed. Anonymous is decentralized and inconsistent by nature — anyone can act in its name — and its signature sign-off endures:

"We are Anonymous. We are Legion.
We do not forgive. We do not forget. Expect us."

THE DARKNET MARKETS — SILK ROAD (2011–2013)

The first modern darknet marketplace, Silk Road, ran on Tor and used Bitcoin to hide buyers and sellers. Created by Ross Ulbricht (alias "Dread Pirate Roberts"), it operated from 2011 until the FBI seized it in 2013, having facilitated more than $200M in mostly illegal sales. Ulbricht was convicted in 2015 and sentenced to life without parole; he was granted a full presidential pardon on January 21, 2025. Silk Road proved both the power and the peril of combining anonymity networks with cryptocurrency — and shaped a decade of law-enforcement focus on the dark web.

08HARDEN · DEFENSE STACK

./harden.sh --all   # the habits that stop most attacks in this terminal

  • Patch everything, fast. OS, browser, phone, apps, routers. Most breaches use bugs that already had a fix.
  • Turn on MFA — app or hardware key, not SMS. A stolen password alone becomes worthless.
  • Use a password manager with unique random passwords per site. Kills reuse, credential stuffing, and most keyloggers.
  • Back up offline with versions (3-2-1). The one true cure for ransomware and wipers.
  • Least privilege. Don't browse as admin/root. Give apps only the permissions they truly need.
  • Encrypt in transit. HTTPS, a trusted VPN on public Wi-Fi, disable auto-join to open networks.
  • Distrust urgency. "Act now or lose access" is the signature of phishing. Slow down and verify.
  • Verify before you run. Official sources, checked signatures/hashes, no "cracked" software.
  • Change default passwords on routers, cameras, and IoT so your gear never joins a botnet.
  • Log & watch. You can't defend what you can't see. Enable logging and alerts.
  • For your own code: validate input, parameterize queries, deny by default, keep dependencies current.
  • Have a plan. Know how you'd disconnect, restore from backup, and rotate credentials before you ever need to.
